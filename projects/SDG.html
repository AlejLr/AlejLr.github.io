SDG Challenge — DSV Electric Fleet Charging

A real-world sustainability and operations challenge for DSV, one of the world's largest logistics companies. Our team designed and proposed a wave-based charging workflow for their electric truck fleet — a solution DSV went on to implement, with initial estimates showing approximately 80% reduction in previously calculated operational costs.

The challenge

DSV operates a growing fleet of electric trucks and faced a set of interrelated operational problems stemming from inefficient charging practices:

  • Driver waiting time: drivers were idling while vehicles charged, creating unnecessary cost and reducing effective working hours.
  • Unnecessary energy use: charging was not aligned with operational schedules, leading to energy waste and off-peak inefficiencies.
  • Battery degradation: uncoordinated charging patterns accelerated battery wear, increasing long-term maintenance and replacement costs.

The challenge was framed around sustainability and operational efficiency: find a solution that reduces cost, extends battery life, and fits within DSV's existing operational structure without requiring large infrastructure investment.

The proposed solution

Our team developed a wave-based transportation and charging workflow that decouples driver working time from vehicle charging time — the core insight being that a truck does not need a driver present while it charges.

  • Wave-based scheduling: trucks are organized into departure and return waves, creating predictable windows when vehicles are available for charging without driver downtime.
  • Next-day operational charging: charging is scheduled according to the next day's route requirements, ensuring trucks carry exactly the charge they need — avoiding both overcharging and undercharging.
  • Driver-vehicle decoupling: drivers hand off vehicles at the depot rather than waiting on-site, freeing working hours and reducing operational overhead.

The solution was designed to integrate with DSV's existing fleet management workflows, minimizing change management friction while delivering measurable efficiency gains.

Results and impact

DSV evaluated the proposed workflow and moved to implement it on their electric fleet. Based on initial estimates at the time of implementation:

  • ~80% reduction in previously calculated operational costs associated with the charging process.
  • Reduced driver waiting time tied to vehicle charging.
  • More controlled charging cycles, reducing battery degradation risk over time.
  • Energy use aligned with actual operational demand rather than ad-hoc availability.

This is the only project in my portfolio where an external organization reviewed the work and committed to implementing it — making it the clearest evidence of real-world analytical and business impact.
